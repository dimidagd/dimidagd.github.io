The essence of estimation

The generation of information that has the following properties

  • quality (accuracy/reliability) higher than the raw measurements
  • contains information not directly available in the measurements

[Multitarget-multisensor Tracking: Principles and Techniques : Yakkov Bar-Shalom Xiao-Rong Li]

  • No access to variables inside the first two blocks
  • The only accessible variable are the measurements which are affected by noise.

The estimator

Uses knowledge and processes the measurements to yield an estimate of a variable of interest(state or state uncertainty), while trying to make best use of the available data. In this process it is taking into account the:

  • System dynamics
  • Sensor Models
  • Prior information & probabilistic nature of the uncertainties

Tracking

Tracking is the processing of measurements or observations obtained from a target in order to maintain an estimate of its current state

The state of a target usually consists of:

  • Kinematics - position, velocity, acceleration , turn rate, etc. expressed w.r.t a coordinate frame
  • Feature components - target classificiation, spectral characteristics, radar cross-section etc.

Measurements or observations are noise-corrupted quantities which are related to the state of a target, such as

  • Direct estimate of position (GPS)
  • Range and azimuth(bearing) from the sensor (radar, cameras)
  • Range rate (radar)

The observations are not raw data sequences but usually the output of a complex signal processing subsystem.

Coordinate systems

Four coordinate systems are usually involved in tracking algorithms.

  • Geodetic
  • Local-tangential
  • Local spherical
  • Earth centered Earth-fixed (ECEF) coordinates

The transformations between the systems exist and are non-linear.

Geodetic to ECEF

GPS measurements usually arrive in geodetic coordinates, where as tracking is usually performed in the local tangential plane. The transformation between these two requires an intermediate transformation from geodetic $(\phi,\lambda,h)$ to ECEF $(X,Y,Z)$ coordinates.

\[ \begin{align*} x = 3 \\ y = 5 \end{align*} \]

\[\begin{align*} X &=(N+h)cos\lambda cos\phi \\ Y &= (N+h)cos\lambda sin\phi \\ Z &= [N(1-e^2)+h] sin\lambda \end{align*}\]

ECEF to local-tangential plane (ENU)

The local-tangential plane is the east-north-up rectangular coordinate system and it is determined by fitting a tangential plane to the surface of the earth at some reference point. The reference point is the origin of the frame. Such a system is convenient in the context of tracking nearby ships because the motion and measurement models reduce to two-dimensional problems.

Given a reference point in Geodetic $[\phi_0,\lambda_0]$ and the corresponding ECEF $[X_0,Y_0,Z_0]$ coordinates, then any other point's ECEF coordinates $[X,Y,Z]$ can be converted to local tangential plane coordinates $[x,y,z]$ using the following transformation

\[ \begin{bmatrix} x\\ y\\ z \end{bmatrix}= \mathcal{L}\left(\begin{bmatrix} X\\ Y\\ Z \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} X_0\\ Y_0\\ Z_0 \end{bmatrix}\right) \]

where $\mathcal{L}$ is a function of the reference point $[\phi_0,\lambda_0,h_0]$

\[ \mathcal{L} = \begin{bmatrix} -sin\phi_0 & cos\phi_0 & 0 \\ -sin\lambda_0cos\phi_0 & -sin\lambda_0sin\phi_0 & cos\lambda_0\\ cos\lambda_0 cos\phi_0 & cos\lambda_0 sin\phi_0 & sin\lambda_0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Body fixed frame (or local tangential own-ship frame)

This frame is the local-tangential frame rotated around the z-axis so that the x-axis is aligned with the own-ships heading direction from the true north $h_{self_{true}}$. This frame in case of a non rolling-pitching vessel coincides with the body-fixed frame. This is also called the sea-keeping frame because the vessel is assumed to move along the direction of its average velocity. Since this system is rotating along the vessel's heading direction and so do the local sensors, it is convenient to use for tracking purposes.

src: [Kinematic Models for Manoeuvring and Seakeeping of Marine Vessels]

The transformation thus is:

\[\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix}_{ok}= R_{z}\]

\[\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix}_{\text{w.r.t. own-ship}} = R_{z}(\frac{\pi}{2}-h_{self_{true}}) \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix}_{\text{local tangential}} \]

where

\[ R_{z}(\theta)=\begin{bmatrix} cos\theta &-sin\theta &0\\ sin\theta &cos\theta &0 \\ 0 &0 &1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Purpose of using different coordinate systems

Both the motion and the measurement model equations depend on the coordinate system of choice. Sensor measurements from a radar usually arrive in the local spherical system, where as the GPS measurements either for the own-ship or for other vessels arrive in the geodetic system. It is convenient to express a target's motion model w.r.t the local tangential body fixed frame, since the local sensors like the radar and the cameras are rigidly attached to the body-frame. This simplifies the measurement models for local sensors, nevertheless it is creating the necessity to simplify the global measurement sensors like the AIS.

Motion model

When choosing a coordinate system for the target motion model, it is very convenient to assume the target's motion on the local-tangential plane with the origin placed on the own-ship(body fixed frame).

The motion models proposed in literature are numerous. Trying to keep the complexity low, linear motion models assume a constant velocity(CV) or constant acceleration (CA). These models have the advantage that the state transition equation is linear. Unfortunately they assume straight motions and are thus unable to model rotational motion. When one introduces the angular speed of the target around the z-axis, the resulting models are referred to as curvilinear models.

Use of the local tangential own-ship coordinate system

Tracking objects using the body-fixed planar frame is a fair assumption at sea since a target in the vicinity of the own-ship in this case can be projected on the self-body fixed frame, and is thus a fair approximation as long as the distance between the tracked target and the own-ship is not large enough for the earth's curvature to induce approximation errors. The model's fidelity using the body-fixed frame is a topic of debate. In the context of sensor fusion and local situational awareness, the on-board sensors used on a ship operate within a range limit that should --- at least intuitively --- ratify the tangential plane approximation.

When it comes to situational awareness in a more global context, i.e. tracking target vessels across the Atlantic, one could argue that there is no sense in applying sensor fusion since the targets are observable solely through GPS measurements.

The above is just an assumption at this point.

State vector

Assuming the target's motion on a plane, then two different state-space descriptions can be used depending on the expression of the velocity vector:

  • Cartesian velocity model \(\mathbf{x} = \left[x,y,\dot{x}, \dot{y},\omega\right]^{T} \)

  • Polar velocity model \(\mathbf{x} = \left[x,y,\upsilon, h,\omega\right]^{T} \)

where

\[ \begin{align*} \upsilon &= \sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2} \\ h &= arctan\left(\frac{\dot{y}}{\dot{x}}\right) \\ \omega &= \dot{h} \end{align*} \]

Where:

  • $(x,y)$ is the displacement relative to the local self-body fixed coordinate system

  • $\upsilon$ is the target's linear velocity

  • $h$ is the heading angle

  • $\omega$ is the turn-rate of the target

all expressed w.r.t the own-ship's body frame which has the x-axis aligned with the own-ship heading direction.

Continuous constant turn rate and velocity (CTRV) model

Starting with a time continuous model

\[\dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = f(\mathbf{x}(t)) + w(t)\]

then assuming that the linear velocity \(\upsilon\) and the turn rate \(\omega\) remain constant --- which is a fair assumption for a moving vessel --- the non-linear dynamics are

\[f = \begin{bmatrix}\upsilon cos(h) \\ \upsilon sin(h) \\ 0 \\ \omega \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}^{T}\]

which is describing constant turn rate and velocity(CTRV)model.

Discretisation of the CTRV motion model

Since the system is simulated in discrete time, one can integrate the motion model within a sample time \(T\) and obtain exactly

\[ \mathbf{x}(t+T) = \mathbf{x}(t) + \int_{t}^{t+T}(f(\mathbf{x}(\tau)+w(\tau) )d\tau \]

and hence the discrete update model by ignoring the noise is

\[ \mathbf{x}(t+T) = g\left(\mathbf{x}(t)\right) \]

where

\[ g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} + \begin{bmatrix} \frac{2\upsilon}{\omega}sin(\omega T)cos(h + \frac{\omega T}{2})\\ -\frac{2\upsilon}{\omega}sin(\omega T)cos(h + \frac{\omega T}{2})\\ 0 \\ \omega T \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} , \omega \neq 0 \]

or

\[ g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} + \begin{bmatrix} \upsilon T cos(h)\\ \upsilon T sin(h)\\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} , \omega = 0 \]

Observation models

An observation model $z_k = h(\mathbf{x_k}) + w_k$ expresses the observations $z_k$ at time instances \(t_k\) given the state vector $\mathbf{x}$ . $w_k$ is assumed to be white gaussian noise with covariance matrix that depends on the specific sensor. The selection of the state vector $\mathbf{x}$ can lead to linear or nonlinear observation models.

Radar observation model

The radar observation model includes calculating the distance and bearing from the own-ship to the target in polar form $z_{radar} = \begin{bmatrix} \rho, \theta \end{bmatrix}^{T}$ , given the target's position $\begin{bmatrix}x_t, y_t\end{bmatrix}^{T}$ w.r.t the sensor's coordinate frame. If the radar's frame is aligned to the vessel's frame, then the radar measurement model can be expressed as:

\[ z_{radar}(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} \rho \\ \theta \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \\ arctan(\frac{x}{y}) \end{bmatrix} + w_{radar} \]

where the measurement noise \(w\) is assumed to be white, zero-mean Gaussian with covariance $R_{radar} = \text{diag}\left[\sigma^2_{\rho}, \sigma^2_{\theta}\right] $

A reasonable sizing of the noise variances can be

  • $\sigma^2_\rho = 10 \textit{m}$
  • $\sigma^2_\theta = 3 \frac{\pi}{180} [rad] $

GPS observation model

The GPS observation model calculates the target's relative position \(\begin{bmatrix}x, y\end{bmatrix}^{T} \) w.r.t the own-ship tangential coordinate frame given

  • The own-ship's geodetic coordinates $\begin{bmatrix}\phi_{0}, \lambda_{0} \end{bmatrix}^{T}$
  • The own-ships sea-keeping heading angle from true north $h_{0_{\textit{TN}}}$
  • The target's measured geodetic coordinates $\begin{bmatrix}\phi, \lambda\end{bmatrix}^{T}$ which is usually available at sea through the Automatic Identification System.

\[ z_{GPS}(\mathbf{x},\begin{bmatrix}\phi_{0}, \lambda_{0}, h_{0_{\textit{TN}}} \end{bmatrix}^{T}) = \begin{bmatrix} x \\\\ y \end{bmatrix}_{GPS} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\\\ y \end{bmatrix} + w_{GPS} \]

The fact that the geodetic coordinates and the heading to true north of the self-vehicle is not included in the state-space representation leads to a linear measurement system, as the measurements are pre-transformed into the local-tangential plane w.r.t the self-vehicles heading direction and the noise is added on the the post-transformed measurements.

The AIS measurements can be transformed into local-tangential vessel coordinates by using the aforementioned transformations.

\[ \begin{bmatrix} x \\\\ y \end{bmatrix}_{\text{local tangential}} = R_z(\frac{\pi}{2}-h_{0_{\textit{TN}}}) \cdot F_1\left(F_2(\begin{bmatrix} \phi \\\\ \lambda \end{bmatrix}),\begin{bmatrix} \phi_0 \\\\ \lambda_0 \end{bmatrix}\right) \]

Where

  • \(F_1\left( \begin{bmatrix} X \\ Y \\ Z \end{bmatrix}_{ECEF},\begin{bmatrix} \phi_0 \\ \lambda_0 \end{bmatrix}\right)\) is the transformation from ECEF to ENU, given the geodetic coordinates of the own-ship $\begin{bmatrix} \phi_0, \lambda_0 \end{bmatrix}^T$ as a reference point.

  • $F_2\left(\begin{bmatrix} \phi
    \lambda \end{bmatrix}\right)$ is the transformation from geodetic to ECEF coordinates

  • $R_z(\theta)$ is the transformation from ENU to sea-keeping frame.

AIS observation model

An alternative method to using the aforementioned GPS model is to leverage the fact that the tracked targets are in the viscinity of the own-vehicle, and thus small differences in geodetical coordinates can be linearly mapped to ENU differences and the other way around. x [src:"Wikipedia:Geographic coordinate conversion"]

By differentiating the model mentioned in the Coordinate systems section, one can confirm that for given own-ship geodetic coordinates $(\phi,\lambda)_{\textit{own-ship}}$

\[ \begin{bmatrix} dE\\dN\\dU \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} (N(\phi)+h)\cos\phi &0 &0\\ 0 &M(\phi) + h &0\\ 0 &0 &1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} d\lambda\\d\phi\\dh \end{bmatrix} \]

Which can be solved for

\[ \begin{bmatrix} d\lambda\\ d\phi \end{bmatrix}= \mathbf{T}(\phi)^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} dE\\ dN \end{bmatrix} \]

Since the target's state is in the sea-keeping frame, one needs to transform it to ENU.

If $\mathbf{R}_z(h)$ is the Euler's angle transformation for a rotation $h$ around the z-axis, then the transformation from sea-keeping to ENU

\[\mathbf{R}^{\textit{ENU}}_{\textit{sea-keeping}} \]

given the own-ship's seakeeping heading from true-north $h_{0_{\textit{TN}}}$ is ,

\[\mathbf{R}^{\textit{ENU}}_{\textit{sea-keeping}} = \mathbf{R_{z}}(\frac{\pi}{2}-h_{0_{\textit{TN}}})\]

\[ \begin{bmatrix} dE\\ dN \end{bmatrix}= \mathbf{R}^{\textit{ENU}}_{\textit{sea-keeping}} \begin{bmatrix} X\\ Y \end{bmatrix}_{\textit{target wrt}_{{\textit{sea-keeping}}}} \]

and thus, by combining the above equations

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \phi\\ \lambda \end{bmatrix}_{\textit{target}}= \begin{bmatrix} \phi_{0}\\ \lambda_{0} \end{bmatrix}_{\textit{own-ship}} + \mathbf{T}(\phi_{0})^{-1}\begin{bmatrix} dE\\ dN \end{bmatrix} + w_{\textit{AIS}}=\]

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \phi_{0}\\ \lambda_{0} \end{bmatrix}_{\textit{own-ship}} + \mathbf{T}(\phi_{0})^{-1} \mathbf{R}^{\textit{ENU}}_{\textit{sea-keeping}}(h_{0_{\textit{TN}}}) \begin{bmatrix} X_{\textit{target}}\\ Y_{\textit{target}} \end{bmatrix}_{\textit{wrt}_{{\textit{sea-keeping}}}}+w_{\textit{AIS}} \]

Where

  • \(\mathbf{T}(\phi)= \begin{bmatrix} N(\phi)\cos\phi &0\\ 0 &M(\phi) \end{bmatrix} \) is the transformation matrix from geodetic differences to ENU differences.

  • \(N(\phi) = \frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{1-e^2\sin^2\phi}}\) is the prime vertical radius of curvature

  • \(M(\phi) = \frac{\alpha(1-e^2)}{(1-e^2\sin^2\phi)^\frac{3}{2}}\) is the meridional radius of curvature

  • \(\alpha,\beta \) are the equitorial radius(6378.1370 km) and the Polar radius(6356.7523 km) respectively, as derived from the WGS-84 ellipsoid model.

  • \(e = \sqrt{1 -\frac{b^2}{a^2}}\) is the ellipsoid's eccentricity.

  • \(h = 0 \) since the own-ship is always floating on the geoid.

  • \(w_{\textit{AIS}}\) is white gaussian zero-mean noise with covariance matrix \(\mathbf{R}_{\textit{AIS}}\) depending on the noise intensity of the AIS source.

The above equation can be rewritten to the standard measurement model formulation

\[ z_{AIS}(\mathbf{x},\begin{bmatrix}\phi_{0}, \lambda_{0}, h_{0_{\textit{TN}}} \end{bmatrix}^{T}) = \begin{bmatrix} \phi\\ \lambda \end{bmatrix}_{\textit{target}} = \begin{bmatrix} \phi_{0}\\ \lambda_{0} \end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x} + w_{\textit{AIS}} \]

, which is a linear measurement model that is approximating the geoid with a spherical-linearization about the own-ship's geodetic location.

Camera model

Assuming a camera sensor and a pinhole model, with the camera z-axis aligned with the own-ship body frame

A top-view of the pinhole model seen in the figure below

Then the measurement model for the predicted pixel location of a target obtained from a deep neural-network classifier and localiser along the y-axis is found by the projecting a point in homogeneous coordinates

\[x_{\text{wrt}_{\text{sea keeping}}} =\begin{bmatrix}x, y, 0, 1\end{bmatrix}^T\]

to the finite camera frame given measured information $z_{gyro}$ about the rotational state of the own-ship w.r.t the sea-keeping frame:

\[ z_{\textit{cam}_{\textit{px}}}(\mathbf{x},z_{\textit{gyro}}) = \frac{\begin{bmatrix}-P_1-\\-P_2-\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{x}}{ \begin{bmatrix} -P_3- \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}}+ w_{\text{camera}} \]

Where $\mathbf{P}$ is the projection matrix and $P_{i}$ it's rows

\[ \mathbf{P} = \begin{bmatrix}-P_1- \\-P_2-\\-P_3- \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{K}\mathbf{\mathbf{R}_{\textit{own-ship}}^{\textit{camera}}}[\mathbf{I} | \mathbf{-C}] \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{\mathbf{R}_{\textit{sea-keeping}}^{\textit{own-ship}}} &\mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} &1 \end{bmatrix}\]

Because of the denominator this is a non-linear model, and a description of the matrices that comprise the projection matrix \(P\) is given below:

\(\mathbf{K}\)

is the intrinsic camera calibration matrix

\[\mathbf{K} = \begin{bmatrix}f m_x &0 &p_x \\ 0 &f m_y &p_y \\ 0 &0 &1 \end{bmatrix} \]

with

  • \(f\) is the intrinsic camera calibration parameter, focal length in mm
  • \(m_x, m_y\) are the camera's sensor pixel density in \(\frac{\text{number of pixels}}{mm}\) across the two different directions of the image plane.
  • \(p_x,p_y\) correspond to the position of the principal point of the image in pixel coordinates
  • \(w_{\textit{camera}}\) is white gaussian zero mean noise with variance related to the classifier accuracy, or any calibration errors in the extrinsic and intrinsic camera matrices.

$\mathbf{R}_{\textit{own-ship}}^{\textit{camera}}$

is the extrinsic camera rotation matrix that describes the orientation of the camera frame w.r.t. the own-ship.

$\mathbf{C}$

is the extrinsic origin of the camera frame w.r.t. the own-ship.

$\mathbf{R}_{\textit{sea-keeping}}^{\textit{own-ship}}$

Is the transformation matrix that corresponds to the roll-pitch rotation of the own-ship w.r.t the sea-keeping frame. Please note that the order of the rotations matters.

\[ \mathbf{R}_{\textit{sea-keeping}}^{\textit{own-ship}}=\left( R_{\textit{pitch}} R_{\textit{roll}}\right)^T \]

Where \(R_{\textit{pitch}},R_{\textit{roll}}\) are the standard rotation matrices for Euler's angles $z_{\textit{gyro}}=(\alpha,\beta)^T = (\textit{roll},\textit{pitch})^T$.

\[ R_{\textit{roll}}= R_x(\alpha) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 &0 &0\\ 0 &\cos\alpha &-\sin \alpha\\ 0 &\sin \alpha &\cos \alpha \end{bmatrix} \]

and

\[ R_{\textit{pitch}}= R_y(\beta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\beta &0 &\sin \beta\\ 0 &1 &0\\ -\sin \beta &0 &\cos\beta \end{bmatrix} \]

A basic assumption at that point, is that the own-ship is able to measure the angles $(\alpha,\beta)$ of the own-ship body frame w.r.t the sea-keeping frame. These angles are usually available on a ship by using one or multiple gyroscope sensors.

Kalman filter

Modeling Assumptions:

  • System's state evolution is linear and driven by
    • Known input
    • Additive process noise which is assumed to be a zero-mean white, uncorrelated process with known covariance \(Q(k)\)
  • Measurements - linear sensor function of the state with
    • Addditive measurement zero-mean white noise with known covariance $R(k)$
  • Initial state , a random variable with known mean(initial estimate) and covariance (initial uncertainty)

src

Under the above assumptions the Kalman Filter is the MMSE state estimator. If the random variables are not Gaussian, but one can still obtain their first two moments, then the KF is still the best linear MMSE estimator.

KF pitfalls in tracking

  • Usually the motion models are nonlinear
  • The measurement models are usually nonlinear
  • Unable to capture mode changes(target maneuveres)
  • Can't handle cross-correlated noise
  • Single target & unimodal

Extended Kalman Filter

The EKF is a suboptimal state estimation algorithm for nonlinear systems.

Modeling Assumptions:

  • Same as the KF except that the motion model and/or the measurement models are nonlinear functions of the state, note that the noises are still assumed to enter additively into the models.
  • First order Taylor expansion of the nonlinear functions are a reasonable approximation to the original functions

Taylor approximation of the CTRV motion models

A nonlinear motion model $x_{k} = f({x}{k-1})$ can be linearised around a known state $x{k-1}$

\[ f_{k-1} \approx F_{k-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \nabla_{x_{k-1}} f_{k-1}^{T}(x_{k-1}) \end{bmatrix}_{x_{k-1} \rightarrow\text{latest estimate}}^{T} \]

and for the CTRV model this leads to

\[ F_{{CTRV}_{k-1}} = \begin{bmatrix}1 &0 &\frac{sin(h+T\omega) - \sinh}{\omega} & \upsilon \frac{cos(h+T\omega)-cosh}{\omega} & \upsilon\frac{sinh -sin(h+T\omega) + T\omega cos(h+T\omega)}{\omega^{2}}\\ 0 &1 &-\frac{cos(h+T\omega) - cos(\omega)}{\omega} &\upsilon \frac{sin(h+T\omega)-sinh}{\omega} & \upsilon\frac{cos(h + T\omega) -cosh + T\omega sin(h+T\omega)}{\omega^{2}}\\ 0 &0 &1 &0 &0 \\ 0 &0 &0 &0 &1\end{bmatrix} \]

similarly, a measurement model $z_k=h(\mathbf{xk})$ can be linearised around the latest motion propagated estimate $x{k}$ as

\[ h_{k} \approx H_{k} = \begin{bmatrix} \nabla_{x_{k}} h_{k}^{T}(x_{k}) \end{bmatrix}_{x_{k} \rightarrow\text{motion updated estimate}}^{T} \]

Thus the radar model that measures distance and bearing(given a CTRV state-space) is linearised as

\[ H = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}} &\frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}} &0 &0 &0 \\ -\frac{y}{x^2+y^2} &\frac{x}{x^2+y^2} &0 &0 &0 \end{bmatrix} \]
